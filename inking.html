01 · What Will It Look Like?

Tattoo Styles

Tattoo art has exploded beyond the flash sheets on parlor walls. Modern inks, techniques, and an influx of formally trained fine artists mean the range of what's possible on skin has never been wider. Here are the major lineages.

Photorealist

Thanks to modern inks and techniques, portraits and images can get far closer to the ideal of "realism" than those from decades past. High-detail shading and color work create lifelike renderings.

Biomechanical

Credit the "Alien" movies with inspiring this branch of surrealism — a combination of human anatomy and robot-like mechanical parts, as though the skin were peeling away to reveal circuitry beneath.

Surrealist

Just like the 20th-century arts movement, this style covers everything from Salvador Dalí-inspired dreamscapes to fantasy monsters and incoherent nightmares. Anything goes — that's the point.

Fine Line Black & Gray

Pioneered in the mid-1970s in Los Angeles, this technique involves subtly shaded, intricate designs and portraits rendered entirely without color. Delicate, detailed, and deeply expressive.

Tribal

A modern U.S. trend that sprang up in the mid-1980s, imitating the bold geometric tattoos common in many ancient tribal cultures. Heavy black linework, symmetry, and patterns that flow with the body's contours.

Asian / Japanese

Large symbolic designs — particularly Japanese — are considered timeless. Koi morphing into dragons are a popular theme for arm sleeves. These pieces often tell stories through traditional iconography and saturated color.

Traditional American

Think Betty Boop, anchors, or "Mom" in a heart. Before the 1970s, this was the only true style in the United States. Bold outlines, limited palette, and designs that read cleanly at any distance. The style is enjoying a strong revival.

Flash

Not a style per se, but the name given to the pre-printed designs on the walls of a tattoo shop. Flash designs are often trendy then outdated — choose wisely. Remember the Tasmanian Devil craze of the 1980s?

Ancient

The oldest known tattoos were black tribal designs found on the "Iceman," a European mummy estimated to be 5,200 years old. A 2,500-year-old mummy found in Siberia also bore elaborate designs — proof that humans have been inking since before recorded history.

02 · How Does It Work?

The Science of Ink

A tattoo machine uses the basic technology of a doorbell — an electromagnet that was first patented in 1891 and has changed remarkably little since 1929. The needle punctures the skin up to 3,000 times per minute, depositing ink a drop at a time, 1–2 millimeters below the surface.

Epidermis

The skin's outer layer acts like a tinted window over the tattoo. Ink passes through it but doesn't stay here — this layer constantly sheds and regenerates.

Dermis — The Target

This is where the ink lives permanently. Antibodies surround the pigment particles and trap them in place within fibroblast cells in the connective tissue. The body identifies the particles as foreign, but they're too large for white blood cells to move or destroy.

Subcutaneous Fat

If the needle reaches too deep into this layer, the ink will immediately spread and blur. A skilled artist maintains precise, consistent depth throughout the entire session — it's what separates good work from great work.

Lines vs. Shading

Small groupings of needles — often three, sometimes just one — draw the sharp, distinct outlines. Larger groups of up to 32 needles are splayed out in rows, like the bristles of a paintbrush, and used for shading and coloring.

Safety Standards

Reputable artists use sterile needles, disposable gloves, and antiseptic creams. Always verify your studio's hygiene practices. Look for an autoclave, single-use ink cups, and a clean workstation before you sit down.

Tattoo Ink Composition

Tattoo ink is small pigment particles suspended in a carrier solution — commonly ethyl alcohol or purified water. Traditionally, metal salts, lead, cobalt, and carbon gave ink its color. Modern formulations have shifted toward organic dyes with plastic-based pigments used in printing inks.

03 · How Much Will It Hurt?

The Pain Map

Pain tolerance varies by person, but in general: the fewer the needles, the more it hurts. Bony areas don't necessarily hurt more — a better indicator is skin softness. Inner thighs, inner arms, and the torso are often extremely tender. Artists say women tend to handle the pain better than men. Tattoos in areas with soft skin tend to be more painful overall.

04 · Where Should It Go?

Placement Strategy

Where you put your tattoo matters almost as much as what you get. Different body areas age, stretch, and fade differently. Think long-term — you're choosing a spot for the next several decades.

Sun Exposure

Sun fades tattoos significantly over time. Plan to use strong sunblock consistently, or choose a placement that's naturally well-covered by clothing.

Aging & Shape

Tattoos in squishier areas like the midsection will lose their shape much more quickly than those on bonier areas. Weight fluctuations compound this effect.

Trendiness

Arm bands and lower back placements were once hugely popular, then fell out of fashion. Consider whether your placement choice is driven by a trend or by genuine personal meaning.

Durability

Tattoos on feet and hands tend to fade or become uneven quickly because those areas get so much daily use, friction, and sun exposure. Expect more frequent touch-ups.

05 · What Happens After?

Aftercare Essentials

Your tattoo is an open wound for the first few weeks. How you care for it during this window determines the long-term quality of the work. Follow your artist's specific instructions — they know their ink and technique best — but here are the universals.

1

Leave the bandage on

Keep your artist's initial wrap on for the time they recommend — typically 2 to 24 hours depending on the bandage type. This protects the fresh wound from bacteria.

2

Wash gently, pat dry

Use lukewarm water and a fragrance-free, antibacterial soap. Pat — never rub — with a clean paper towel. Repeat 2–3 times daily for the first two weeks.

3

Moisturize with intention

Apply a thin layer of unscented moisturizer or your artist's recommended aftercare product. Too much suffocates the skin; too little lets it crack. A light, consistent film is ideal.

4

Don't pick, don't scratch

Peeling and itching are normal during healing. Picking scabs pulls ink out of the dermis and creates patchy, faded spots. Let everything shed naturally.

5

Avoid submersion & sun

No pools, hot tubs, oceans, or baths for at least 2–3 weeks. Keep the tattoo out of direct sunlight. Once healed, apply SPF 30+ sunscreen to preserve color and definition for years.

06 · What If I Hate It?

Removal & Reality

Laser treatment is the recommended method — it works by breaking pigment particles into fragments small enough for the body's immune system to clear. But it's not magic: removing a tattoo is typically more painful than getting one, takes between 6 and 15 sessions spaced weeks apart, and can cost thousands of dollars. The skin may never return to its original state — a faint ghost of the design often remains.

The best strategy? Take your time on the front end. Research your artist, sit with the design for months, and choose placement thoughtfully. A tattoo you love is one you'll never need to remove.
